## Safely Applying Machine Learning in Robotics Projects

Robotics and machine learning are both promising and exciting frontier fields, and when combined, they become even more fascinating, with an effect far greater than the sum of their parts. Therefore, when we apply machine learning in robotics projects, it is easy to become overly excited and try to use machine learning to achieve many results that were previously only imaginable. However, applying machine learning in robotics is very different from using machine learning directly. One important difference is that general machine learning systems primarily cause direct impact in the virtual world, whereas machine learning systems in robots can easily cause direct impact on the physical world through the robot. Therefore, **when we apply machine learning in robotics projects, we must always pay attention to system safety**, ensuring that the safety of both developers and users is reliably guaranteed during product development and throughout the product's market lifespan. Moreover, not only commercial projects need to consider safety; personal projects also require ensuring safety. No one wants to cause irreparable regret to themselves or their friends/colleagues due to a safety oversight.

The above is not alarmist. Let us consider the following scenarios:
Suppose you are developing a mobile cargo robot for your company's logistics warehouse, designed to operate in the same working environment as workers to promptly help them transport goods to their destination when needed. This robot has a visual pedestrian detection system to identify whether there are people ahead. When the robot encounters obstacles while moving forward, this pedestrian detection system participates in determining the robot's behavior. If there is a person, the robot will choose to take a wide detour to avoid pedestrian obstacles on its path; if there is no person, the robot can take a narrow detour to avoid obstacles. However, if the pedestrian detection system makes a detection error one time, failing to detect that the obstacle ahead is a worker organizing goods on a ladder, it chooses to take a narrow detour. When the robot approaches, the worker suddenly discovers a robot approaching, becomes startled, and falls directly in front of the robot's path. Considering that logistics warehouse cargo robots typically weigh at least several hundred kilograms including their load, the consequences of an actual collision would be unthinkable. If such a situation truly occurs, the commercial prospects of this robot product would be destroyed, and the company and its responsible persons would be held accountable (even in the legal sense). More importantly, the harm caused to the victim and one's own guilt would severely affect both parties' lives.

Not only commercial projects are affected. Suppose you are developing a small entertainment robotic arm to try to help you complete some small tasks on the desktop, such as moving a teacup or flipping switches. Your robotic arm also relies on an object detection system to identify task targets. One time while moving a teacup, the robotic arm fails to detect a power strip in the planned path, causing the teacup to accidentally fall and spill water into the power strip, causing a short circuit. If you are lucky, you might only need to replace the power strip, but if unlucky, it could even cause a fire or electric shock. I believe no one would want to encounter such emergencies.

Therefore, regardless of what kind of robotics project we are applying machine learning to, we must always pay attention to and ensure system safety.

### Methods for Ensuring Safety: Risk Assessment and Independent Safety Systems

#### Risk Assessment

To ensure the safety of robotics and machine learning systems, we first need to know what potential dangers exist. We can achieve this through Risk Assessment.
There are already many articles online about how to complete a risk assessment, so we will not go into too much detail here. What we want to emphasize is that for identified risks, we need to provide a risk mitigation plan whenever possible. More importantly, we need to ensure the actual execution of these plans, rather than merely providing plans on paper and calling it done. An unexecuted plan is equivalent to no plan.

#### Independent Safety System

After understanding the potential risks, we can mitigate the robot system-related portion of those risks by designing an independent safety system.
Specifically, this safety system should be independent of the machine learning system and reside at the lower levels of the robot architecture with sufficient or the highest level of priority. In fact, this safety system should not target only the machine learning system but should address all aspects of the entire robot. In other words, when developing robotics projects, there must be a sufficiently safe and independent safety system. The safety measures for the machine learning system are merely a partial manifestation of this independent safety system being "sufficiently safe."
Let us return to the earlier example of the logistics warehouse mobile cargo robot. If the robot's wheels have independent safety circuits and automatically brake when power is cut, and the robot has a LiDAR that strictly complies with safety standards and also has a safety circuit, with this LiDAR's safety circuit directly connected to the wheel safety circuit, then regardless of whether the robot detects a person ahead or someone suddenly enters the robot's path, the LiDAR will detect the foreign object and directly cut power to and brake the wheels through the independent safety circuit, ensuring no collision occurs. Such a configuration is completely independent of any control logic and thus unaffected by any upper-level system. **For developers, when we have a reliable independent safety system, we can confidently use the latest breakthrough technologies without worrying about whether new technologies might cause unexpected consequences.**

### Ethical Issues in Machine Learning Systems

In addition to the fundamental safety issues discussed above, ethical issues in machine learning systems can also affect the use of robots.

For example, the classic ethical issue of imbalanced racial representation in training datasets. Let us return to the earlier example of the logistics warehouse mobile cargo robot. If our training dataset contains only images of Asian people, when we want to expand into overseas markets, our overseas users may very well find that our robot cannot effectively recognize their workers. While an independent safety system can prevent accidents, emergency stops in front of workers certainly do not make for a good user experience. The overseas sales of our robot would also be affected.

Ethical issues in machine learning systems are currently a hot topic of discussion. As industry professionals, we need to stay informed about the latest developments in this area. On one hand, this means incorporating these considerations into system design from the beginning; on the other hand, we hope that our achievements can bring happiness to more people rather than causing them trouble.
